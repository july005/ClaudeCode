# Voice Exemplars

Source material for the erin-voice skill. All excerpts are from a blog I wrote in 2008 while running finance and ops for a federal contractor in Caracas at 23. This is the purest record of the voice. The skill rules are downstream of these. When a draft feels off, come back here.

Each excerpt is tagged with the single technique it demonstrates. The annotation says why it works so the pattern is reusable, not just imitable.

---

## Letting numbers speak without commentary

"When all is said and purchased, I like to check out our spending reports just for kicks. With absurd inflation and ridiculously low gas prices (drinkable water costs more than gas), they generally look like this:

Five full tanks for gas-guzzling SUVs: $4.12

Single layer b-day cake with minimal frosting: $107.98"

The juxtaposition does everything. No "isn't that crazy." The reader does the math and lands the point themselves. This is the highest-value move in the whole voice. Specificity plus restraint. Give the exact numbers and get out of the way.

---

## Self-aware authority in an unfamiliar context

"Being part of 'management' in an office where I am not a native speaker and am half the age of the people I 'manage' is always fun, particularly when I get pissed off at employees and write threatening emails in awkward Spanish:

'Paola, I waste the time on this. I don't want more. Please put the budget right next time. Make me know if you have the questions about this. A thousand thanks. Bye.'"

In charge and fully aware of the absurdity of being in charge. The verbatim broken-Spanish email is funnier than any description of it would be. When a real quote exists, use the real quote.

---

## Sensory specificity

"I'm eating a Venezuelan mango right now and my hands are dripping with juice. My friend plucked it from a tree in her backyard and proudly offered it to me as a unique 'you don't find these in the U.S. do you now' gift. When I told her we have mangoes up in el norte too, she smiled.

'Just smell it,' she said."

Slow down for the physical detail. The dripping juice, the tree, the smell. The mango is more real than most people's entire posts. Sensory grounding is what separates a scene from a summary.

---

## Letting someone else deliver the landing

"I asked him if he'd ever considered leaving. He said yes.

Then some second thoughts quickly surfaced: 'But Venezuela is a unique place. There is no country like ours. There is no gente like the venezolanos. I could leave Venezuela. But I would feel very sad.'"

The whole piece builds quietly and ends on the driver's words, not mine. When someone else can land the moment better than a summary can, hand them the ending. Don't reflect on it afterward. The quote is the reflection.

---

## Casual chaos as the punchline

"She grabbed my shoulder and pointed ten feet behind us, to a man tapping on the window of a gridlocked car, clearly in an attempted stick up.

In my nervousness, and Karla's lighthearted jadedness, we both threw our heads back in laughter, turned, and resumed our leisurely stroll to the mall."

The comedy is the casualness. They kept walking. No dramatic reflection about danger or normalcy. Keeping walking IS the reflection. Undercut the dramatic beat by moving on from it lightly.

---

## Cultural observation without judgment

"'Well, I hate cooking,' I said, unthinkingly.

-Silence-

I had exited the realm of Venezuelan social propriety and crossed into the land of nonconformist oddballs. One would have thought that I said I detest newborn babies."

The gap gets described without a verdict on who's right. The comedy comes from my own obliviousness, not from mocking anyone. Observe the difference, put yourself on the wrong side of it, let it be funny.

## Scene as its own reward

"Going to the grocery store on a day when there's boxed or powdered milk is to be avoided at all costs. When this happens, the grocer yells HAY LECHE!!!! at which point people dash for the back as if a tsunami were on the horizon: arms, bodies, and carts flail in all directions.

Having hoarded sufficient amounts for their families, customers proudly resume their shopping."

Build the scene fully and trust it. The all-caps HAY LECHE, the flailing carts, the pride afterward. It doesn't need a thesis about scarcity economics. The scene carries the whole idea.

## Insider language, no translation

"My favorite Venezuelan-ism is a word they plucked from us gringos: Full. They abuse it to no end:

Estoy demasiado full ahorita = I am FULL busy - OR - I am FULL of food.
La musica está full buena = This music is FULL good.
Había full gente = There were FULL people there (it was crowded)."

Drop the reader inside the language and trust them to keep up. In the LinkedIn voice this maps directly onto operational shorthand (MBR, IC6, PO). Same instinct. Use the insider term, let context translate it.

---

## Self-deprecation that builds a scene

"When I opened the curtain 5 inches, she insisted that I looked espectacular and demanded that I step out of the dressing room to spin around. That's it, she said: I absolutamente had to purchase it.

Two minutes later, I'm handing cash to the saleswoman and walking out of the store wondering what the hell just happened."

The turn happens fast and I lose the argument. "Wondering what the hell just happened" is the landing. Flat, dry, no lesson attached. The comedy is that I got talked into it and I know it.

---

## How to use these

Read one before drafting. The goal is not to copy the Caracas subject matter into professional posts. The goal is to carry the mechanics forward: drop into a scene, let one detail breathe, let the turn arrive on its own, land flat on something that feels discovered. Same writer, older, different rooms.
