**WORKFLOW:** Purchase order lifecycle, vendor engagements
**SOURCE:** User A, senior program lead, six vendor engagements. Discovery interview conducted by E.

## STATED OUTCOME

Real-time visibility into every PO — original amount, spent, committed, remaining — so change orders can be approved in the meeting instead of two days later. She asked for this three times. She described the benefit entirely in terms of her own time: "hours a week," "the thing that wakes me up at night."

## OUTCOME I WOULD CHECK

That is a local outcome. It reduces her effort and, on her own account, changes one thing downstream: she would approve more change orders. She said so out loud — "I'd approve more of them, probably."

The workflow is attached to a budget line and a set of vendor contracts. What it is actually for is *delivering engagements inside a committed spend envelope*. The person who owns that is finance, and finance signs off on what she tells them and does not know what the work was. Nobody has ever asked what an engagement costs end to end, and nobody can answer it — the finance system issues a new PO number per change order, so one engagement and its four change orders read as five unrelated purchases.

**So the first finding is not a workflow finding. Nobody owns the outcome this workflow serves.** Finance owns the money and has delegated judgment to the person spending it. She owns the delivery and has no view of the money. The gap between those two is where every problem in this transcript lives.

If the real outcome is spend control rather than her calendar, the dashboard is not just insufficient — it is pointed the wrong way. It removes the only friction currently limiting change-order approval, and it does so before anyone has established whether that friction is doing useful work.

## SEQUENCE

1. Identifies a need, scopes with vendor → User A → verbal/working scope
2. Vendor sends quote → Vendor → quote document
3. Submits PO request → User A → PO request
4. Approval (auto under threshold; director then finance above) → Finance/Director → approved PO
5. Vendor works → Vendor → deliverables
6. Scope changes → User A, her stakeholders, or the vendor → a change in the ask
7. Vendor flags the overrun (some do; some just invoice at the end) → Vendor → email, sometimes a formal document
8. Determines whether there is room: spent, committed-not-invoiced, remaining budget line → User A → a number she does not have
9. Emails finance for the position → User A → request; two-day wait
10. Tells vendor to proceed before approval → User A → verbal go-ahead
11. Reconstructs what was agreed, three weeks later, from email → User A → a reconciliation
12. Matches vendor invoice line items to original PO scope lines → User A → a mapping only she can make
13. Finance signs off → Finance → approval of what she told them
14. Change order issued as a new PO → System → an unlinked purchase record

## SEAMS

→ **Step 6 to 7:** the change happens before anyone records that it happened. There is no artifact at the moment of change, only at the moment of billing. Everything downstream is reconstruction.
→ **Step 7 to 8:** the trigger arrives as unstructured email, sometimes not at all. Fidelity and, in the silent-invoice cases, timeliness are lost entirely.
→ **Step 8 to 9:** the position she needs is split across four sources with different refresh rates — the finance system (lags), invoiced spend (lags), committed spend (in her head and her inbox), overall budget (a monthly spreadsheet, stale on arrival). Time is lost here, and so is accuracy, which she does not mention.
→ **Step 9 to 10:** two days of wait, resolved by proceeding without approval. Accountability is lost here. The work starts unauthorized and the paperwork is bent to fit afterward.
→ **Step 12 to 13:** finance signs off without knowing what the work was. The control does not attach to anything. Accountability is lost a second time, formally this time.
→ **Step 13 to 14:** the engagement's identity is lost. Five POs, one project, no thread.

## LOAD-BEARING

→ **Step 12, matching invoice line items to PO scope lines.** She is the only person who can do it, and she knows it: *"I know that the 'Phase 2 delivery' line is the workshops, because I remember the conversation where we split it that way. If someone else looked at it they'd have no idea."* Asked whether an error would be caught: *"Probably not."* This is a system with a human inside it. She cannot articulate the rule because there is no rule — there is a memory of a room. Do not automate this in the first pass. An AI mapping here would produce confident matches with no one able to check them, and the check does not currently exist.

→ **Step 9, the two-day wait.** This is the one that matters and the one nobody would flag. The delay is not just cost. It is the only functioning control on change-order approval in the entire workflow. She named it herself: *"some of them I push back on just because I don't know if I have the money, so I stall... Some of that pushback is accidental. It's not that I decided no. It's that I couldn't find out fast enough to say yes."* And the stalled ones frequently resolve without spend — *"Sometimes they just absorb it. Sometimes we realize we didn't need the extra thing."*

The friction is absorbing a decision nobody has claimed. Remove it and you have not removed a bottleneck; you have removed the brake, and you have handed the accelerator to the person whose stated intention is to approve more. The week-five question answers itself here: in week five you would find out that change-order spend went up and no one decided that it should.

## WHAT I WOULD DO FIRST

Not the dashboard.

I would answer the two questions she cannot answer, because they determine whether the dashboard should exist at all. **First: what does an engagement actually cost, end to end?** Stitch the parent PO to its child change-order POs across her six engagements and total them. She says this is a day per vendor by hand; it is a clean retrieval-and-matching job over data that already exists and is already trusted, and it is the single highest-value thing in this transcript — because it lets her compare the cheap vendors who generate change orders against the expensive ones who do not, and she has *never added that up.* **Second: what caused each change order?** The answer is sitting in her email. Classifying historical change orders by origin — vendor under-scoping versus internal scope creep — is retrieval and synthesis over available sources, which is the definition of an AI problem. Going forward, the reason field is an ops fix: make it structured, make it required, name who reviews it.

Those two answers are perhaps two weeks of work and they cost almost nothing. They also settle the question. If most change orders trace to internal scope creep, then the dashboard accelerates the wrong thing and the real fix is a change-control decision upstream. If most trace to vendor under-scoping, the fix is a scoping standard in the contract, and *then* a position-visibility tool has something honest to sit on. Right now she is asking me to build a faster route to a decision that should be made less often rather than faster.

## WHAT I WOULD NOT TOUCH

The line-item reconciliation, and the two-day wait.

The reconciliation is the judgment in this workflow and it lives entirely in one person's memory of conversations that were never written down. It is exactly the step that looks most automatable from the outside, and there is no one who could check the output. It can be redesigned — but that means making the mapping explicit at the moment the scope is split, which is a different project, upstream, and slower.

The wait, I would not touch for a different reason. It is the only thing standing between this budget and every change order a vendor cares to raise, and it is doing that job by accident. Before anyone removes it, someone has to decide who approves change orders and against what — and that decision does not exist today. Removing accidental friction before you have built the deliberate control is not enablement. It is a spend increase with a dashboard on top.

## WHAT I STILL NEED TO KNOW

→ **What does an engagement cost end to end**, and are the "expensive" thorough vendors actually more expensive? She has never added up the change orders.
→ **How often does a stalled change order resolve without spend?** She says "more often than you'd think" and does not track it. This is the size of the brake I am telling her not to remove.
→ **Who owns change-order approval?** The transcript shows finance signing off on information it does not have and her deciding under time pressure with information she does not have. That is not an approval process; it is two people each assuming the other one checked.
→ **What is finance's sign-off supposed to catch?** Ask the controller, not her.
→ **What is the actual auto-approval threshold, and do change orders re-test it?** If each change order gets its own PO number, a sequence of small change orders may be routing around a threshold that a single accurate PO would have hit. The transcript does not say. It should be the first thing checked.
→ **What is the deadline pressure attached to?** She said the project "existed to hit" a deadline. That is the structural commitment this workflow serves, and I do not yet know who set it or what happens if it slips. That answer changes the weight of everything above.
