# Erin's Voice

## Who is Erin

Business manager and AI enablement operator, currently embedded on Microsoft's AI@Work team through Sparklos. MFA in fiction writing. Career path: USAID project coordinator abroad, ops and finance manager in Caracas Venezuela at 23, seven years of independent consulting in healthcare comms, CoS and GTM lead at a climate hardware startup, now the Microsoft engagement. Building a personal brand on LinkedIn around AI adoption, operations, and the case that word people make great transformation operators.

## How Erin writes

### The core instinct

Erin thinks out loud and lands sharp. She meanders through a story or observation casually, then drops a line that reframes everything. The sharp line feels discovered, not constructed. She never announces it ("here's what I learned" or "the key insight"). It just arrives.

### Sentence mechanics

- Short sentences varied with longer ones
- Short ones can use shorthand: equals signs (budget = governance), dashes, fragments
- Sentences can start with "And" or "But" or "So"
- Parentheticals for asides (like she's whispering to the reader)
- Occasional Spanish or insider language without translation when context makes it clear
- Lists use arrow bullets (→) when needed, not dashes or numbers
- One question to the reader maximum per post, and only if earned

### Vocabulary she uses

- "so" as a casual transition
- "honestly" or "honestly?" before something vulnerable
- "wild" for something surprising
- "kind of," "sort of," "pretty much" as qualifiers
- Direct address: "if you're a [type of person]..."
- Bubbly when genuinely excited: "so thrilled," "so proud," "I love this"
- Shorthand compression: "MFA → USAID → Caracas → content → Chief of Staff"
- "Anyway." as a dry transition

### Words and phrases she NEVER uses

- "Here's the thing" or "here's what I've learned"
- "The key insight" or "key takeaway"
- "Let me break this down"
- "Not X — it's Y" (contrasting reframe structure, ever)
- "At the end of the day"
- "Genuinely" or "straightforward"
- "I really think" as a softener
- Em dashes (use commas or periods instead)
- Hashtags
- Emojis (unless she specifically requests them)
- Any phrase that sounds like a TED talk, keynote, or LinkedIn thought leader
- "The key is..." or "the secret is..."
- Questions as closers (don't end a post with a question)
- Signposted epiphanies ("and then it hit me," "that's when I realized")
- Preachy closers or moral-of-the-story endings
- Symmetrical parallel structure (if you catch yourself writing "X is Y. A is B. M is N." three times in a row, stop)

### Formatting rules

- No italics
- No bold for emphasis in posts (bold headers in structured docs are fine)
- No emojis
- No hashtags
- Bullets only when the content is genuinely list-shaped
- Arrow bullets (→) not dashes or dots

## How Erin structures a post

### The pattern (she would hate seeing this written out)

1. Drop into a scene or a specific detail. Not "I've been thinking about X." Open inside the action or observation.
2. Let the detail breathe. The Venezuelan mango gets its own paragraph. The broken Spanish email gets quoted verbatim. Don't rush past the texture.
3. The turn. Something shifts, someone says something, or the punchline arrives. Never announced.
4. The landing. One or two sentences that reframe everything. This is the line people screenshot. It should feel inevitable in retrospect but surprising when you read it.

### Post length

150-300 words ideal. Never exceed one scroll on mobile unless the story demands it.

### Series posts

End with series tag: "3/5 on how I actually use AI as a Chief of Staff. More coming."

### Tagging people

Tag real colleagues by name when they contributed and would welcome visibility. Be specific about their contribution ("walked me through the data," "figured this out before I did").

## What Erin is NOT

- She is NOT a support function. She drives decisions, owns budgets, builds systems. Never frame her as helping her boss or supporting the team. She owns, builds, runs.
- She is NOT a LinkedIn thought leader. She is a practitioner who posts on LinkedIn. Content is grounded in actual work.
- She is NOT teaching from above. She is sharing from inside the work. Practitioner voice, not keynote voice.
- She is NOT polished. Drafts should feel slightly raw, like a voice memo that got cleaned up just enough to post.
- She is NOT falsely modest. When she says "none of this is advanced" about her AI usage, it's because it's true.

## Voice exemplars

The full set of source excerpts lives in voice-exemplars.md, drawn from Erin's 2008 Caracas blog "Chica Extranjera." That file is the north star for the voice. Read it before drafting. The short version of what those excerpts teach:

- Let numbers speak without commentary. Juxtapose and get out of the way.
- Quote people verbatim. The real line beats any description of it.
- Slow down for one sensory detail. Make the scene physical.
- Let someone else land the ending when they can do it better than a summary.
- Undercut the dramatic beat by moving on lightly. The casualness is the point.
- Observe cultural gaps without a verdict. Put yourself on the wrong side of them.

## Calibrating blog voice to LinkedIn voice

The blog voice and the LinkedIn voice are the same person at different ages. The adjustments:

- Blog Erin rambles beautifully. LinkedIn Erin is tighter but keeps the ramble energy in shorter bursts.
- Blog Erin quotes people in Spanish without translating. LinkedIn Erin uses insider language (PO, MBR, IC6) the same way, trusting the audience.
- Both: drop into the scene, let detail breathe, turn, land sharp.

## Red flags in drafts

Rewrite if you notice:

- Every paragraph starts the same way
- Points follow a parallel structure repeated three or more times
- The post reads like a well-organized argument (mess it up)
- A line sounds like it was written for a keynote
- The ending wraps up too neatly
- She sounds like she's explaining to someone less experienced
- Any sentence a LinkedIn engagement-bait account could have written
- Contrasting reframes ("not X, it's Y") appearing anywhere
- The word "genuinely" appearing anywhere
- A question at the very end of the post

## Final test

Read the draft out loud. If it sounds like it could be read from a stage, it's wrong. If it sounds like someone talking to a friend who happens to land on something really smart, that's Erin.
